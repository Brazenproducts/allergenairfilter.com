Pollen vs Dust — Two Different Enemies

Same air filter, but pollen and dust don't behave the same way. Understanding particle size, source patterns, and how each moves through your home changes which filter you should actually be buying.

Pollen: Seasonal and Outdoor

Pollen is a seasonal problem driven by plant reproduction cycles. Tree pollen peaks in early spring, grass pollen in late spring and early summer, and weed pollen in late summer into fall. In most temperate climates, you're dealing with some form of pollen from March through October.

But here's what many people miss: pollen doesn't just drift in through open windows. It attaches to shoes, clothing, pet fur, and gets tracked inside repeatedly throughout the day. If you or anyone in your home goes outside during peak season, pollen is entering your home every single time someone walks through the door.

Pollen grains are relatively large particles — typically 10 to 100 microns. That's actually good news for filtration. A standard MERV 8 pleated filter captures the majority of pollen grains. Moving up to MERV 11 or MERV 13 gives you progressively more complete capture, but you're not starting from zero with a basic filter.

Key insight

During peak pollen season, the biggest gains come from washing hands and changing clothes when you come inside — not just from upgrading your filter. That said, a MERV 11 filter will capture what does get in.

Dust: Year-Round and Indoors

Dust is a continuous, indoor problem. It comes from dead skin cells, fabric fibers, pet dander, dust mite feces, cooking residue, and tracked-in soil. Unlike pollen, dust doesn't have a seasonal cycle — it's generated every day inside your home.

Dust particles range from about 0.5 microns up to 20 microns. The smaller the particle, the more dangerous it is to respiratory health and the harder it is to capture. Dust mite feces — one of the most allergenic components of household dust — is in the 1 to 10 micron range, which puts it in a filtration category that requires MERV 11 or better.

Pet dander is similarly problematic. It's not the pet hair itself that's the allergen — it's the skin flakes, dried saliva, and urine residue that become airborne. Dander particles are typically 2 to 20 microns, with the smallest being the most allergenic and the hardest to filter.

Which MERV Handles Each Problem Best

For pollen-only concerns (primarily seasonal, outdoor-driven):

  • MERV 8 — Adequate for light pollen seasons. Catches large pollen grains easily. Budget-friendly choice if you don't have indoor dust or pet issues.
  • MERV 11 — The practical upgrade. Handles pollen plus the smaller dust and dander particles. Most homeowners with mild allergy concerns land here.
  • MERV 13+ — Overkill for pollen alone unless you live with severe allergy sufferers or in a high-pollen region. The airflow restriction may not be worth the marginal gain for pollen.

For dust and dander (year-round concerns):

  • MERV 8 — Does not reliably capture dust mite feces or fine pet dander. You may feel no improvement despite changing filters regularly.
  • MERV 11 — The practical minimum for dust and pet allergen control. Captures the majority of problem particles in the 1–10 µm range.
  • MERV 13 — Significantly better for fine particles. Recommended if someone in the home has confirmed dust mite or pet allergies.

If you have both pollen and dust concerns — which describes most households — MERV 11 is the most common recommendation. It handles both without the airflow penalty of MERV 13 in standard residential systems.

Watch out

Do not jump straight to MERV 13 because of pet allergies or pollen season. Check your HVAC system's MERV maximum rating in the owner's manual. Putting a MERV 13 in a system rated for MERV 8 will reduce airflow, strain the blower, and can cause coil freeze-ups in summer.
