MERV Ratings Explained — And Why Bigger Isn't Always Better

MERV stands for Minimum Efficiency Reporting Value. It's a standard that tells you what percentage of particles at specific sizes a filter catches. Understanding it means you're not guessing at the filter aisle — or paying for performance you can't use.

What MERV Actually Measures

MERV is tested by ASHRAE Standard 52.2. A filter is challenged with particles across six size ranges (0.3 to 10 microns), and the resulting efficiency curve determines the MERV rating. Higher MERV means the filter catches a greater percentage of smaller particles across the board.

Key point: MERV tests particle penetration — not whether a filter "feels thick" or looks dense. Two pleated filters with the same MERV rating can have very different airflow characteristics depending on media type, fold depth, and filter size.

Changing from a MERV 8 to a MERV 13 in a system designed for MERV 8 is one of the most common causes of reduced HVAC performance that homeowners never connect to the filter change.

MERV 11 in Practice

MERV 11 is the most commonly recommended filter rating for residential allergy control, and for good reason. It captures the vast majority of the particles that cause indoor allergy symptoms — including pollen grains, dust mite feces, and most pet dander. At 1 micron and above, MERV 11 typically achieves 85–95% efficiency.

The practical advantage of MERV 11 over higher ratings is airflow compatibility. Standard residential furnaces and air handlers (up to 5-ton capacity) are typically rated for MERV 11 or MERV 13. Many older or smaller systems are only rated to MERV 8. A MERV 11 filter will fit comfortably in most systems without measurable airflow loss when the filter cabinet is correctly sized.

One thing to check: MERV 11 filters come in 1", 2", and 4" depths. A 1" MERV 11 pleated filter loads faster than a 4" MERV 11 of the same media because the deeper filter has more surface area. If you're replacing a standard 1" filter, a 4" MERV 11 will often last 3–4x longer before needing replacement.

MERV 13 in Practice

MERV 13 captures smoke particles (including cooking and wildfire-related PM2.5), bacteria, and the finest dust and allergen particles. If someone in the home has asthma or confirmed respiratory allergies, MERV 13 is the minimum recommended rating from most allergists and pulmonologists.

The tradeoff is real. MERV 13 filters have measurably higher airflow resistance than MERV 8 or MERV 11. In systems not designed for MERV 13, this shows up as reduced airflow at the vents, longer run times, and — over months — potential blower motor wear. The filter cabinet and blower motor rating must both support MERV 13 for this to work correctly.

MERV 13 also loads faster in certain conditions — particularly in homes with pets or heavy cooking grease in the air. A filter that looks clean after 2 months may already be at significant pressure drop if it's in a high-particulate environment.

The Airflow Compatibility Check

Before buying any filter above your system's rated MERV, do this:

  1. Find your HVAC install manual or look up the model number online. Most manufacturers specify a maximum filter MERV rating and a minimum filter depth.
  2. Check the return filter grille — some grilles have a MERV rating stamped on the frame or on the filter itself.
  3. Look at the cabinet. If your filter slots in from a side panel rather than a front-access door, you're limited to whatever depth the cabinet allows.
  4. If your system is more than 15 years old and has never had a filter above MERV 8, do not jump to MERV 13. The system was never balanced for that airflow load.
Warning sign

If your home has cold rooms in winter despite the thermostat setting, or certain vents blow noticeably weaker than others after a filter upgrade, your filter may be restricting airflow too much. Test by temporarily re-installing the old filter and comparing the airflow.
