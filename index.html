Complete Buying Guide — What to Know Before You Buy

True HEPA vs. HEPA-Style — Know the Difference

True HEPA filters must capture 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns — this is a tested, certified standard. 'HEPA-style,' 'HEPA-like,' or 'HEPA-type' filters are marketing terms with no certification requirement. For allergy sufferers, only true HEPA certification matters.

Match CADR to Your Room Size

CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) measures how quickly a purifier cleans a room. For effective allergen control, look for a CADR at least 2/3 of your room's square footage. A 300 sq ft bedroom needs at least a 200 CADR. Running an undersized unit at max speed constantly is both louder and less effective than properly sizing it.

Activated Carbon for Odors — When You Need It

HEPA filters capture particles but don't address odors, VOCs, or gases. If you have pets, cook frequently, or live near traffic, a combined HEPA + activated carbon unit removes both allergen particles and chemical odors. The carbon layer needs replacement separately from the HEPA filter.

Filter Replacement Cost — Calculate It Before You Buy

The purchase price is only part of the cost equation. HEPA filters typically need replacement every 6–12 months depending on air quality and usage. Some units cost $20 for a replacement filter; others cost $60+. Check filter price and availability before buying the unit.

Noise Levels Matter for Bedrooms

Air purifiers run continuously for maximum effectiveness, so noise level is a real quality-of-life factor. Look for units with a dedicated sleep mode under 25–30 dB. For context, 30 dB is roughly the level of a quiet library — most people can sleep through it easily.

Placement Matters as Much as the Unit

Place your air purifier in the room where you spend the most time sleeping — typically the bedroom. Keep it at least 2 feet from walls for unobstructed airflow. Don't hide it in a corner. An optimally placed modest purifier outperforms a high-end unit tucked away behind furniture.

Frequently Asked Questions

Allergen Air Filter — Your Questions Answered

What does HEPA actually mean for allergy filters?

True HEPA (High Efficiency Particulate Air) is a tested standard requiring 99.97% capture of particles at 0.3 microns — the hardest size to capture. This includes most pollen, dust mite debris, mold spores, and pet dander. 'HEPA-style' or 'HEPA-like' filters have no certification requirement and may perform significantly worse.

How quickly does an air purifier reduce allergen levels?

A properly sized true HEPA purifier typically reduces airborne allergen levels by 50–80% within 30–60 minutes in a closed room. Full effectiveness comes after continuous operation. Running the purifier on a schedule or only occasionally significantly reduces its effectiveness.

Do air purifiers help with pet allergies?

Yes — pet dander is one of the most common indoor allergens and one of the most effectively captured by HEPA filtration. For households with pets, LEVOIT makes specific filter variants for pet dander. Running the purifier in the room where pets spend the most time delivers the best results.

What's the difference between HEPA 13 and HEPA 14?

Standard HEPA (H13) captures 99.97% of particles at 0.3 microns. HEPA 14 captures 99.995%, including ultrafine particles down to 0.1 microns that standard HEPA misses. HEPA 14 matters most for wildfire smoke, ultrafine diesel particles, and very fine mold spores.

How often should I replace an air purifier filter?

Most manufacturers recommend every 6–12 months depending on usage and air quality. Higher pollution environments, homes with pets, or heavy year-round use may need replacement every 3–6 months. Many modern units have filter life indicators — trust those over a fixed schedule.

Can air purifiers remove mold spores?

Yes — true HEPA filtration effectively captures airborne mold spores. However, a purifier doesn't eliminate mold at its source. If you have active mold growth, addressing the moisture problem and physically removing the mold is necessary. The purifier reduces airborne spread during and after remediation.

Is it worth buying an air purifier with a smart app?

Smart features like air quality monitoring, filter life tracking, and scheduling are genuinely useful if you'll use them. The auto mode — which adjusts fan speed based on detected air quality — is the most practical feature. If you want to set it and forget it, a unit with an auto mode is worth the extra cost.

Should I run my air purifier 24/7?

Yes, for maximum effectiveness. Modern HEPA purifiers are designed for continuous operation and consume only 15–50 watts on lower settings — comparable to a light bulb. The filtration is most effective when running continuously rather than in bursts.

Do air purifiers help with COVID and viruses?

True HEPA purifiers capture virus-sized particles that float on aerosols. The EPA and CDC both acknowledge that air purifiers with true HEPA filtration can reduce airborne virus concentrations. They're not a substitute for ventilation and other precautions but add a meaningful additional layer.

What's the best room size for a bedroom air purifier?

For a standard 200–250 sq ft bedroom, a unit with 150–200 CADR is appropriate. For primary bedrooms or open layouts, size up to 300+ CADR. It's always better to slightly oversize — you can run a larger unit on a lower, quieter setting and still achieve excellent air changes per hour.
