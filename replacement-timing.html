When to Replace Your Air Filter — Season by Season

"Change every 3 months" is the most repeated advice in HVAC — and it's wrong for a lot of households. Your filter replacement schedule should match your actual indoor air quality load, not a calendar default.

Why the "3 Months" Rule Misses the Point

The 3-month interval is a rough average for a standard disposable 1" filter in a moderate-use home. It was never meant to be a universal schedule — it's a marketing convention that became conventional wisdom.

Here's what actually determines how fast your filter loads up:

  • Airborne particle load — pollen season, wildfire smoke, construction dust, pet dander all add particle mass at rates far above normal.
  • HVAC runtime — a home in a hot climate where the AC runs 8–10 months a year loads filters faster than a temperate-climate home with moderate heating use.
  • Filter depth and media type — a 1" pleated filter has far less surface area than a 4" or 5" media filter. Same air quality load, but capacity differs enormously.
  • System airflow design — systems with high static pressure (long duct runs, multiple bends) load filters faster because air is pushed through harder.
  • Return filter grille size — smaller return grilles create a bottleneck effect that loads the filter unevenly and faster.

A filter changed every 90 days in a Phoenix home with heavy AC use and dust storms is severely overdue. A filter changed every 90 days in a mild-climate, single-occupant home with no pets may barely be touched at the 90-day mark.

A Practical Seasonal Schedule

Here's a better framework for timing replacements. Adjust the interval up or down based on your specific conditions.

Season Primary Allergen Typical Interval (1" disposable) Recommended Adjustment
Late Winter / Early Spring Mold spores waking up, dust accumulation 90 days Check filter — may already be loaded from winter heating season
Peak Spring Pollen Tree + grass pollen 60–75 days Replace before season starts, then mid-season. This is the single most impactful filter change of the year.
Summer Grass pollen continuing, dust, mold 60–90 days Heavy AC use loads filters faster. Humidity can encourage mold in the ductwork.
Late Summer / Early Fall Weed pollen, ragweed, mold 60–75 days Second peak replacement timing. Often skipped because "summer's over" but allergens persist.
Winter Heating Dust, indoor air, dry conditions 90 days If you run a fireplace or baseboard heating alongside HVAC, particles may be elevated.

Note: Highlighted rows indicate peak allergy seasons — these are the two most important filter changes of the year.

How Pets and Renovations Change Timing

If you have pets, the standard 90-day schedule is almost certainly too long. Pet dander is one of the finest and most allergenic indoor particles, and homes with dogs or cats cycle a surprising amount of allergen material through the air every day.

  • 1 dog or cat: Reduce interval to 60–75 days. Consider MERV 11.
  • 2+ pets: Reduce to 45–60 days. A 4" deeppleat filter will last significantly longer per change than a 1" standard.
  • Pet allergy sufferers: Check the filter at 30 days visually. If it's visibly loaded at 30 days, your system is working hard and allergen removal is being compromised.

Renovations — even small ones like painting a room, installing flooring, or new cabinetry — produce extraordinary particle loads for weeks to months after the work is done. Sawdust, joint compound dust, and finish off-gassing are all captured by the filter but load it rapidly.

  • During active renovation: Check the filter every 2 weeks. Replace as needed.
  • Post-renovation: Replace immediately when the work finishes. Then again 45–60 days later.
  • New furniture: Particle off-gassing is real for the first 4–6 weeks. Watch for new dust accumulation.

Signs It's Time to Replace Before the Interval

Filter intervals are a guide, not a guarantee. Watch for these performance-based signals:

  1. Allergy symptoms uptick — If family members start sniffling, sneezing, or experiencing itchy eyes and there's no change in outdoor pollen counts, the filter may be loaded.
  2. Visibly dark filter — If the filter has turned noticeably gray or brown and it's been less than 60 days, it's working hard. Hold it up to light — if you can't see light passing through most of the surface area, replace it.
  3. Weaker airflow at vents — Reduced airflow from supply vents when the fan is on is a classic sign of a loaded filter. This also increases HVAC run time and energy cost.
  4. Higher than normal energy bills — A filter at high pressure drop forces the blower to work harder. If your energy bill is up and the weather pattern hasn't changed, the filter is a good place to look first.
  5. Furnace lockout or reduced heating/cooling — Some modern HVAC systems will reduce output or shut down entirely if the filter is causing excessive static pressure. Check the filter before calling a technician.
